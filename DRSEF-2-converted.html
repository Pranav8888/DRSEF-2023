A Simple Mathematical Model to Optimally Determine School Attendance Zones and Student Capacity Growth

Pranavanath Balamurali Independence High School, Frisco

Abstract

The proposed mathematical model determines the optimal school attendance zones and can provide key insights for school capacity plan- ning activities like selecting the right school for student capacity addi- tion and choosing the best location to build a new school. The model uses a linear optimization framework, based on the geographical distri- bution of the student residences, the school locations, and the school student capacity limitations. This simple but effective model can be used by the school administration as a mathematical aid to decide the student attendance zones and evaluate their future school capacity improvement plans. The solution insights can be coupled with other strategic objectives to make a final decision.


  1. ‌Introduction

    In a school district, determining the school attendance zones and planning for future student capacity increases are important tasks performed by the school district administration every year. These complex tasks require con- sidering multiple key factors like the geographical distribution of the student residences, road traffic, land availability, current and future expected student capacity, and availability of essential services etc. In order to help with this activity, a simple but effective optimization model using linear optimization techniques is proposed below. This mathematical model and the resulting solutions serve as a tool in the toolbox, that can assist the administration in the planning process.

    The chosen objective function for the model is to minimize the aver- age distance traveled by the students from their residences to their assigned schools, subject to the current available student capacity of each considered school. This general model provides a lot of value and can act as a sim- ple building block for more complicated models in future that can consider additional requirements or constraints like per-grade student counts and school capacities, actual road and street information, teacher availability and scheduling, grouping students in same communities together etc.

    These kind of linear optimization models are very popular in supply chain network design and optimization and greatly reduces logistics and


    inventory costs. Some problems solved by linear optimization models include determining optimal location of warehouses, plants, and suppliers, deciding on amount of raw material required based on demand and price, identifying the best distribution of the finished products to consumers in time etc.


  2. ‌Linear Optimization Model

    The key data elements and their terminology used in the model are

    The set of students is indexed by T and an individual student is de- noted by t

    The set of schools is indexed by S and an individual school is denoted by s

    The distance from the school s to the residence of student t is indicated as dists,t

    Note: To keep it simple, the distances used in the model are geographi- cal distances based on latitude/longitude of the locations. Actual road distances can be used if obtained from the Google Maps software API.

    The binary decision variable that indicates whether the student t is assigned to school s or not is given by Ys,t. If Ys,t = 1, then student t is assigned to the school s. If Ys,t = 0, then student t is not assigned to the school s.

    The objective function of this optimization problem is given below: Minimize the average distance from the student residences to their assigned schools. This is equivalent to minimizing the total distance of all student residences from their assigned schools

    Min Σ Σ dists,t × Ys,t

    s∈S t∈T

    ΣΣ

    Note that the sigma sign Σ indicates summation and the letters under the sign provide the quantities that are being summed up. The ∈ symbol

    stands for “element of”.

    Next, constraints to the solution are specified as below:

    Every decision variable Ys,t is binary, it can only be either 0 or 1.

    Ys,t ∈ {0, 1}; ∀s ∈ S, ∀t ∈ T

    where ∀s ∈ S means for every school in the set of all schools and

    ∀t ∈ T means for every student in the set of all students.


    To prevent a trivial all-zero solution, every student is to be assigned to a single school

    Σ Ys,t = 1; ∀t ∈ T

    s∈S

    In addition, each school s can only be assigned to a total of students not exceeding its maximum available student capacity Cs

    Σ Ys,t ≤ Cs; ∀s ∈ S

    t∈T

    Putting all these above equations together, the linear optimization model is formulated as below

    Min Σ Σ dists,t × Ys,t

    s∈S t∈T

    subject to

    Ys,t ∈ {0, 1}; ∀s ∈ S, ∀t ∈ T


    Σ Ys,t = 1; ∀t ∈ T

    s∈S


    Σ Ys,t ≤ Cs; ∀s ∈ S

    t∈T

  3. ‌Data and Software

    The data required to build and analyze this optimization model are the ge- ographical locations of the student residences and the schools and the avail- able student capacities of all considered schools. The city of Frisco has made residence addresses available for public use in their Frisco GIS Hub (https:

    //geodata-frisco.hub.arcgis.com/maps/address-pt). This data includes 65,577 land address records with their latitude/longitude information.

    Below as shown in the picture (Figure 1), 10000 random address records from the Frisco GIS database are selected as the student residences used for the simulation analysis. Also the 11 high schools in Frisco school district


    (leaving out the newly started Emerson High school which did not have any residence address locations nearby in the Frisco GIS database ) are considered for the analysis.

    Figure 1



    The freely available ‘lpsolve’ package in R software (https://cran. r-project.org/web/packages/lpSolve/index.html) is used to solve the optimization problem. The ‘lp’ function from the ‘lpsolve’ package solves general linear and integer optimization problems.

  5. ‌Conclusions

This paper proposes a simple mathematical optimization framework that can aid the school district administration greatly as the number of schools and the student population increase immensely. It can provide important insights about forming optimal student attendance zones and future school capacity planning. Different model usage scenarios were evaluated in this paper using R software. This general model can be further extended as required to include any additional information and constraints for serving new use cases.
